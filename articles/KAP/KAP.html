KAP

(Kite Aerial Photography)

"KAP" involves hoisting a camera by means of a large kite. The art has been around for over a hundred years. In older times various mechanical timers were used to trigger the camera shutter, but these days we can employ all sorts of remote controlled solutions.

I took this picture near Santa Cruz, CA.

My 9' delta kite and homemade winding spool.




Radio Box:

The designs began with a cardboard mockup and progressed into a custom wooden box. From there, I added the stripped down electronics into the box one by one.

The controls from left-to-right...

  1. Shutter pushbutton
  2. Focus lock (like pushing shutter button to half-way point)
  3. High/low rate for turning
  4. Left/right push pool for rotating gimbal
  5. Up/down angle of camera
  6. Video Rx and screen power switch
  7. Battery indicator
  8. Main power switch



Gimbal:

For the Z axis rotation, I modified a micro servo for continuous rotation.

The tilt servo goes from +40deg above horizon to -100deg horizon.

A recent modification was to make the head of the picovet suspention collapsible for easier transportation.




Electronics:

This circuit is a schmitt trigger. It reads the pwm from the rc reciever and turns it into a binary signal that controls the camera shutter.

Information on the usb control for my sony a5100 can be found here.

All four of these circuit boards need to be strapped to the gimbal frame. The end result is sloppy yet functional and light weight. In future revisions I will be cleaning that up.

To see what the camera feed from the ground I use the same TX and RX drone racers use displaying on a cheap car-reversing lcd.

On the gimbal, this device is used to downsample the hdmi signal to composite analog. To prevent blocking the usb port on the camera, this short hdmi extender was also used.

Here you can see the whole video system operational with airgap.




Stratospool:

This particular spool design is call a "stratospool". It's a design that's been around since the 70's and has been adapted by many kite enthusiests over the years. I gleaned a lot of info from the KAP forum before designing and building my own.
